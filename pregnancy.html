How to prepare for a baby, trimester by trimester

Pregnancy is dated from the first day of your last period, which is why you are counted as "four weeks pregnant" at your first missed period. Across roughly forty weeks the work falls into three natural stages — get through the tiredness of the first, use the energy of the second, and prepare in the third. The order below is the same one the free printable checklist follows.

First trimester (weeks 1–13): confirm and protect

The first trimester is mostly invisible to everyone else and often the hardest — nausea and exhaustion peak here. There is little to buy yet and a few things that genuinely matter:

  • Start a prenatal vitamin with folic acid as soon as you know
  • Book your first prenatal (booking) appointment, usually around week 8–10
  • Have the dating scan around week 12 and confirm your due date
  • Note foods and medicines to avoid, and stop alcohol and smoking
  • Decide who you want to tell, and when

Second trimester (weeks 14–27): the window to get things done

Energy usually returns and the nausea eases, which makes this the best stretch for planning, paperwork and any travel. The anomaly scan around week 20 is the big milestone.

  • Attend the anomaly scan (around week 20) — and find out the sex if you want to
  • Tell your employer and plan maternity or parental leave
  • Book antenatal classes; they fill up early
  • Start the nursery and the bigger purchases while you still feel mobile
  • Take a photo of your bump each week — it goes faster than you think

Third trimester (weeks 28–40): pack, plan, wait

Appointments come closer together and the focus shifts to the birth itself. Have everything ready by around week 36, because babies do not read the calendar.

  • Write a flexible birth plan and talk it through with your midwife
  • Fit and check the car seat well before the due date
  • Pack the hospital bag by week 36 (yours, baby's, and a partner's)
  • Wash and put away the first newborn clothes
  • Agree who to call, the route to hospital, and the backup plan
The one thing worth doing early: fit the car seat and read its manual weeks before you need it. It is the one item you legally cannot leave the hospital without, and the one nobody wants to be learning at 3 a.m. with a newborn in their arms.

Pregnancy questions people actually ask

When does each trimester start and end?

The first trimester runs from week 1 to the end of week 13, the second from week 14 to the end of week 27, and the third from week 28 until birth. Because pregnancy is dated from your last period, you are counted as two weeks along before conception even happens.

When should you announce a pregnancy?

Many people wait until the end of the first trimester, around week 12–13, once the risk of miscarriage drops and the first scan is done. There is no rule — some tell close family early for support, others wait past the 20-week scan. Do what feels right for you.

What should you pack in a hospital bag?

Pack by around week 36. For you: ID and maternity notes, a going-home outfit, loose pyjamas, dark towels, toiletries, a phone charger and snacks. For baby: bodysuits, a sleepsuit, a hat, a blanket and a car seat fitted in advance. For a partner: a change of clothes, cash for parking and a charger. When do you feel the baby move?

First-time parents usually feel flutters between 18 and 22 weeks; in a second pregnancy it can be as early as 16 weeks. In the third trimester your midwife may ask you to notice the pattern of movements, because a clear change is always worth a call.

How many prenatal appointments will I have?

A typical first pregnancy has around ten checks: a booking appointment near week 8–10, a dating scan around week 12, an anomaly scan around week 20, then visits that grow closer together through the third trimester. Higher-risk pregnancies are seen more often.

What should you eat in the first trimester?

Aim for small, frequent meals if nausea hits — dry crackers, toast, ginger and cold foods are often easier than hot cooked meals. Keep protein, whole grains, fruit and vegetables coming, and take a prenatal vitamin with folic acid (400µg) and vitamin D. Avoid unpasteurised cheese, raw or undercooked meat and fish, high-mercury fish and alcohol. If food won't stay down at all, tell your midwife — it can be treated.

Is it safe to exercise while pregnant?

For most healthy pregnancies, yes — around 150 minutes a week of moderate activity is recommended. Walking, swimming, prenatal yoga and stationary cycling are all good. The rule of thumb is that you should be able to hold a conversation while moving. Avoid contact sports, anything with a fall risk, and lying flat on your back after the first trimester. Always clear it with your midwife if you have any complications.

How do you write a birth plan?

A birth plan is a one-page note of your preferences, not a contract — keep it flexible. Cover where you want to give birth, pain relief you would like to try or avoid, who will be with you, positions for labour, and preferences for after birth (skin-to-skin, feeding, who cuts the cord).
